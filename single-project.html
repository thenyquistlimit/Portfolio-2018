Before Development

Meet and understand the business needs of the User, Client or Both. Usually this will involve the spelling out of the user needs at a high level. At this point in time the relevant Business people have their say. This might include the Product Owner, Business Analysts who are client facing, Sales, Marketing, Development and Test Teams.

As the product is designed for one specific set of business users - a data entry team working for a bank with the product hosted on an internal intranet, we don’t usually create personas. This would differ on a publicly facing website or app.

Looking at these types of teams we can deduce that keyboard accessibility is very important, given that they are used to filling out forms left to right.

At this stage I would normally have a whiteboard session or do a rough sketch of a proposed design. This can include brainstorming certain ideas, design thinking, information architecture, and requirements gathering. System constraints would act as guardrails in the design process too and these would be discussed with the relevant stakeholders.

I would then create a Confluence page to collate the requirements and add pictures of early sketches and high level business needs. It stands as a central information point for the development and test teams so they get early visibility on upcoming work.

At this point I would decide whether this piece of functionality would be best served by a low fidelity prototype on paper or Balsamiq, high fidelity prototype in UXPin, or code a design with HTML, CSS and Javascript and show objects like graphs etc.

When this is completed and we are happy it meets the Business Requirements and covers User Needs, we do some Hallway Usability testing to determine if a user that is not familiar with the product can carry out basic tasks such as navigating the system, and carrying out small tasks.

The feedback is then analyzed and checked against Nielson’s Heuristics, elements of Cognitive Psychology, Ease of Use. Etc. Findings are ranked most important first down to least important.

At this stage the prototype is updated to reflect the findings as this is the cheapest time to initiate change. The beauty of using a tool such as UXPin, or Sketch with Avocode or Zeplin is that we can use the prototype to drive the micro requirements with the Development team. Both allow me to to create and export css stylesheets that detail assets, the colour palette, typography and layout sizing.

These can be added to the development stories and can cut down time spent on the CSS side of things, giving development one less thing to worry about. At this stage the development story is ready to be taken on.

During Development

During the development phase I liaise with development and test teams to provide feedback, coding solutions as required, advice on accessibility for both keyboard and color, hierarchical layout, visibility of system statuses, labelling, and cross browser compatibility issues.
